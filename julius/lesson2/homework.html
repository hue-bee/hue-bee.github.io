Part C — ELA: Reading Passage

Read the passage once for understanding. Then annotate: circle signal words, underline the main idea of each paragraph. Answer all 6 questions.

The Grid That Grew: How Urban Planners Designed New York City's Streets

[¶1] In 1811, a group of city commissioners published a plan that would shape New York City for centuries to come. First and foremost, they imposed a strict rectangular grid on the island of Manhattan — a network of evenly spaced avenues running north-south and streets running east-west. At the time, most cities had grown organically, with winding lanes that followed old farm paths or natural features. Manhattan's grid was a radical departure.

[¶2] The commissioners chose the grid for practical geometric reasons. A rectangle is the most efficient shape for dividing land: every block has a calculable area, every property line forms a right angle, and perimeter measurements are straightforward. As a result, developers could quickly determine how much frontage a building lot had or how many square feet it contained — calculations that would have been far more complex on irregular lots.

[¶3] However, the grid was not without its critics. Some argued that the perfectly rectangular blocks left no room for diagonal routes, making it inefficient to travel across the city at an angle. Others pointed out that the geometry ignored the natural landscape — rocky outcroppings, streams, and hills that would be costly to flatten. The grid, its critics said, valued mathematical order over practical terrain.

[¶4] The commissioners responded by making adjustments. For example, they preserved Broadway, an ancient Lenape trail that cuts diagonally across the grid, creating famous triangular intersections such as Times Square and Flatiron Square. These triangular spaces — technically composite figures formed where two street systems collide — became some of the city's most iconic gathering places.

[¶5] In contrast to Manhattan's rigid grid, neighborhoods like Greenwich Village and the Financial District developed before 1811 and kept their irregular, winding streets. Today, navigating these older neighborhoods feels entirely different from walking a numbered grid — the irregular block shapes make it much harder to estimate distances or areas at a glance.

[¶6] The 1811 Commissioners' Plan remains a vivid reminder that geometry is never purely abstract. The choice between a rectangle and an irregular polygon, between a grid and an organic street network, shapes how millions of people move through space every single day. Every block in Manhattan is, at its core, a geometry problem that a city decided to solve once — and live with forever.
