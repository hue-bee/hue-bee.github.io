Part 1 — Perimeter

The fence around the yard. The track around the field. Just add every side.

📐 Perimeter

The "Walk Around" Rule

🔑 The Golden Rule
Perimeter = sum of ALL sides. No formula to memorize — just add every edge you can see.
📦 Rectangle Shortcut
P = 2(l + w)
Two lengths + two widths. Or just add all 4 sides — same result.
🔺 Other Polygons
Add every labeled side. Missing a side? Opposite sides equal in a rectangle — use that!
💬 If a square has perimeter 28 cm, what is one side? How do you know?

Part 2 — Area: Rectangles & Triangles

How much carpet do you need? How much paint? That's area — the space inside.

🟦 Area

Rectangle = Rows of Tiles · Triangle = Half a Rectangle

📦 Rectangle
A = length × width
Think: 4 rows × 6 tiles per row = 24 tiles. Units are always squared (cm², ft²).
🔺 Triangle — The "Half" Trick
A = ½ × base × height
Every triangle is exactly half of a rectangle with the same base & height. Draw the rectangle to see it!
💬 A triangle has base 10, height 6. Before calculating — is the area more or less than 30? Why?

Part 3 — Parallelograms & Trapezoids

The slant is a trap — always use the height, not the slanted side.

🔷 Parallelogram & Trapezoid

Slide It Over — The Height Trick

⬡ Parallelogram
A = base × height
Imagine sliding the slanted end straight down — you get a rectangle! Height = perpendicular drop, NOT the slant.
🔹 Trapezoid — Two Different Bases
A = ½(b₁ + b₂) × h
Average the two parallel sides, then multiply by the height. Step 1: add b₁+b₂. Step 2: ÷2. Step 3: ×h.
⚠️ SHSAT Trap!
Problems give you both slant side AND height. Always use the perpendicular height. The slant is bait!
💬 Trapezoid with bases 4 and 10. What is the "average base"? Can you see why we average?

⚠️ SHSAT Traps

The 5 Mistakes That Cost Points

🪤 Trap 1: Using the slant side as height in a parallelogram or trapezoid. Height must be perpendicular — straight up and down.
🪤 Trap 2: Forgetting to write units². Area is always in squared units. Perimeter is just units (feet, not feet²).
🪤 Trap 3: Adding all sides when you need the area. Remember: Perimeter = add sides. Area = formula.
🪤 Trap 4: For composite figures, not finding the missing dimension first. Label ALL sides before calculating anything.
🪤 Trap 5: Forgetting the ½ in the triangle formula. Every single time: A = ½ × base × height.

📖 ELA · Signal Words

Signal Words Are the Author's Road Signs

➡️ Sequence / Order
First, Next, Then, Finally, Subsequently
Tells you the ORDER things happen.

Because, Therefore, As a result, Consequently, Thus

↔️ Contrast
However, In contrast, On the other hand, But, Yet, Although
What comes AFTER flips direction.
➕ Addition / Support
Furthermore, In addition, Also, Moreover, Additionally
More evidence or details piling on.

The Hidden Geometry of City Parks

[¶1] When city planners design a public park, they rely on geometry in ways that most visitors never notice. Every lawn, fountain basin, and walking path is the result of careful calculations involving perimeter and area. Without these measurements, cities could not budget for fencing, paving, seeding, or landscaping.

[¶2] First, planners calculate the perimeter of the park's outer boundary to determine how much fencing or hedging is needed. A rectangular park that is 200 feet long and 80 feet wide, for example, requires 560 feet of fencing along its perimeter. In addition, interior paths that cut through the park must also be measured so contractors know how much pavement to order.

[¶3] However, perimeter alone does not capture the full picture. Area — the amount of flat space enclosed — determines how many bags of grass seed are needed, how much concrete fills a plaza, and whether a playground is large enough to serve a neighborhood safely. A park that looks wide from the street may actually have a smaller usable area than it appears, especially when fountains, benches, and planting beds reduce the open space.

[¶4] Many parks include composite shapes — areas made by combining rectangles, triangles, and other figures. As a result, planners must break large spaces into smaller, simpler shapes, calculate each area individually, and then add the results together. A park with an L-shaped lawn, for instance, can be split into two rectangles whose areas are calculated separately and then summed.

[¶5] In contrast to large city parks, pocket parks — small green spaces tucked between buildings — present a different challenge. Because their boundaries are often irregular, planners must use the formula for trapezoids or even divide the space into triangles to obtain an accurate area measurement. A small error in these calculations can mean ordering far too much or too little material.

[¶6] Ultimately, the difference between a beautiful, functional park and a poorly planned one often comes down to the precision of the geometry used in its design. Planners who master perimeter and area are not just solving math problems — they are shaping the way communities live, play, and breathe.
