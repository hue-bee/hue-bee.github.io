🗺️ 3-Step Annotation Strategy (Use This on Every Passage)

Step 1 — Circle Signal Words
As you read, circle every signal word (however, first, as a result, in contrast, for example). They are clues to structure.
Step 2 — Label Each ¶
In the margin, write 3–5 words summarizing what each paragraph does: defines, gives example, contrasts, explains cause, concludes.
Step 3 — Name the Structure
After reading all paragraphs, ask: "What is the author's overall pattern?" Choose: Sequence · Compare/Contrast · Cause/Effect · Problem/Solution · Definition.

Read the passage using the 3-Step Annotation Strategy. Circle signal words as you read. Then answer all 8 questions.

The Two Faces of the Amazon: Abundance and Fragility
Original passage for SHSAT practice · ~320 words · 6 paragraphs

[¶1] The Amazon rainforest is often described as the "lungs of the Earth." Covering more than 2.7 million square miles across nine South American countries, it produces roughly 20 percent of the world's oxygen and absorbs enormous quantities of carbon dioxide each year. For centuries, scientists and explorers have marveled at its staggering biodiversity — more than 40,000 plant species, 1,300 bird species, and 3,000 types of fish call the Amazon home.

[¶2] However, the Amazon's reputation for abundance can obscure a deeper truth: it is far more fragile than it appears. The forest's soil is surprisingly thin and poor in nutrients. Most of the nutrients in a rainforest are locked inside the living plants themselves — not in the ground beneath them. As a result, when trees are cut down or burned, those nutrients vanish quickly, and the exposed soil can become useless for farming within just a few years.

[¶3] Deforestation is the Amazon's most pressing threat. First, loggers clear land to harvest valuable hardwoods like mahogany. Then, ranchers convert the cleared land into cattle pastures. Finally, when the degraded soil can no longer support grazing, farmers often move deeper into the forest, repeating the cycle. Between 2000 and 2020, the Amazon lost an area of forest roughly the size of Texas — approximately 270,000 square miles.

[¶4] In contrast to the destruction caused by large-scale deforestation, many indigenous communities have lived within the Amazon for thousands of years without causing lasting damage. Their land management practices — rotating crops, harvesting selectively, and avoiding soil exhaustion — have allowed the forest to regenerate continuously. Scientists studying these communities have found that the areas they manage often contain higher biodiversity than surrounding unmanaged regions.

[¶5] Governments and environmental organizations have proposed several solutions. Some advocate for stricter enforcement of existing deforestation laws. In addition, several international agreements now pay countries to preserve their forests as carbon sinks. For example, Brazil's Amazon Fund has received over one billion dollars from Norway and Germany to finance conservation efforts.

[¶6] Ultimately, the fate of the Amazon depends on whether the world can reconcile economic development with ecological preservation. The forest's abundance is real — but so is its fragility. Losing the Amazon would not just be a regional tragedy; scientists warn it could trigger a cascade of global climate effects that no country would be immune to.

Step 2 — Comprehension Questions

Q1. What is the central idea of this passage? [Main Idea]

(A) The Amazon should be completely protected from human activity of any kind.
(B) The Amazon is both remarkably abundant and dangerously fragile, and its survival requires balancing development with preservation.
(C) Deforestation in Brazil is caused primarily by the logging industry.
(D) Indigenous communities are the only groups successfully managing the Amazon rainforest.

Q2. What is the overall organizational structure of the passage? [Structure]

(A) Strictly chronological — events presented in the order they happened
(B) Problem/solution combined with compare/contrast
(C) Pure definition — the passage only defines the Amazon ecosystem
(D) Single cause and single effect only

Q3. In ¶3, the signal words "First… Then… Finally…" indicate that the author is describing… [Signal Words]

(A) three contrasting perspectives on deforestation
(B) a sequence of steps in the deforestation cycle
(C) the causes and effects of climate change
(D) three proposed solutions to deforestation

Q4. According to ¶2, why does clearing Amazon forest make the soil useless for farming quickly? [Supporting Detail]

(A) The soil is damaged by the heavy machinery used in logging.
(B) Nutrients are stored in the living plants, not the ground, so removing trees removes the nutrients.
(C) Rainforest soil is made of sand, which cannot hold water or nutrients at all.
(D) Cattle ranching introduces chemicals that permanently poison the soil.

Q5. Based on ¶4, what can be inferred about why scientists study indigenous land management? [Inference ⚠️]

(A) Scientists want to prove that indigenous people have lived in the Amazon longer than previously believed.
(B) Their sustainable practices may offer lessons that could help preserve biodiversity elsewhere.
(C) Indigenous communities are lobbying governments for legal recognition of their land rights.
(D) Scientists believe rotating crops is the most effective method of carbon storage.

Q6. The phrase "In contrast" at the start of ¶4 sets up a contrast between… [Signal Words / Structure]

(A) the Amazon's past and its present state
(B) deforestation by large-scale industry and sustainable land use by indigenous communities
(C) two different scientific theories about soil nutrients
(D) logging companies and cattle ranchers

Q7. In one sentence, describe the overall text structure of this passage and identify two signal words that helped you determine it. Use the format: "This passage uses _____ structure because it _____. Two signal words that show this are _____ and _____." [Short Answer — Structure]

Q8. The author ends ¶6 by warning of "a cascade of global climate effects." What does this detail prove about the main idea? Write 1–2 sentences using this format: "This detail proves _____ about the main idea because _____." [Short Answer — Detail → Main Idea]
