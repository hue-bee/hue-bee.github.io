Read This Poem

My father drew straight lines for a living —
property edges, fence rows, the angle
at which a wall must lean to hold its ground.
I thought it was the dullest work on earth.

He told me once that every angle has a name:
acute for sharp things, obtuse for the wide,
supplementary when two halves make a flat line,
complementary when two pieces fill a corner.

I laughed. He kept his ruler straight and said
nothing more than: everything leans at some angle —
the trick is knowing which rule it follows.

Now I teach my own students about lines.
I write the same four families on the board
and I hear, beneath my chalk, my father’s voice,
patient as a plumb line, waiting to be right.
