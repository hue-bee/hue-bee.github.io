Read This Poem — “The Carpenter’s Triangle”

He built the roof the way a right triangle
insists on being built — no argument,
no flexibility, just the clean logic
of a² and b² agreeing to become c².

He never read the theorem in a book.
He learned it from the lumber’s weight,
from the way a ladder always leans
at the angle that doesn’t slip.

My grandfather was the hypotenuse —
the longest side, the one who bore the load,
stretched tight between two points
the rest of us couldn’t reach alone.

Now I solve for c every Tuesday morning
and think: he already knew the answer.
He just used different words for it —
hold, trust, don’t let it slip.
