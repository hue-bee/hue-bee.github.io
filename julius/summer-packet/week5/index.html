Reading — Informational Passage

📖 Read the passage once for understanding. Annotate: circle signal words, underline the main idea of each paragraph. Answer all 5 questions.

[¶1] New York City uses more than one billion gallons of water every day. That water travels from reservoirs in the Catskill Mountains through a network of tunnels bored through bedrock — some more than 700 feet underground. The oldest tunnels, City Tunnel No. 1 and City Tunnel No. 2, were completed in 1917 and 1936. For decades, engineers could not inspect or repair them because there was no backup tunnel to divert the flow. If either tunnel failed, millions of people would lose their water supply.

[¶2] The solution was City Tunnel No. 3 — the largest capital construction project in New York City history. Work began in 1970 and the first section opened in 1998. As a result of this new capacity, engineers were finally able to perform inspections of the older tunnels for the first time in over 70 years. They found cracks, leaks, and worn valves that needed urgent repair. Without Tunnel No. 3, these problems could never have been safely addressed.

[¶3] The scale of the project required extraordinary precision in measurement. Each tunnel section must fit exactly: a gap of even a few centimeters would allow water to seep into the surrounding rock under enormous pressure. Engineers used both metric and customary units, converting constantly between the two as equipment and specifications arrived from different countries. A single misread measurement — inches confused for centimeters — could cost weeks of work and millions of dollars.

[¶4] City Tunnel No. 3 will not be fully complete until the 2020s. However, the project demonstrates a core principle of infrastructure engineering: the best time to solve a problem is before it becomes a crisis. By building a new tunnel before the old ones failed, the city ensured that a billion people would continue to drink clean water — without ever knowing how close the system came to collapse.
