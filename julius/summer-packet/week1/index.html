Reading — Text Structure

📖 Read the passage once for understanding. Annotate: circle signal words, underline the main idea of each paragraph. Answer all 5 questions.

[¶1] Every year, engineers who design city water systems face the same invisible problem: how to move millions of gallons of water from reservoirs to kitchens, hospitals, and fire hydrants — quietly, reliably, and without anyone noticing. The solution is a hierarchy of pipes. The largest mains carry water in units of hundreds of thousands of gallons. As a result, smaller branches split off and carry that flow into neighborhoods. Finally, the smallest lines deliver exact quantities to each building — often measured in fractions of a gallon per second.

[¶2] The challenge is not just distance, but unit translation. A pressure reading might be given in pounds per square inch; a pipe's diameter in millimeters; a daily usage figure in millions of gallons. Engineers must therefore convert constantly — between imperial and metric, between rates and totals, between small-scale measurements and city-wide flows. A single conversion error, in contrast to a simple calculation error, can cause a pipeline to overpressure and burst.

[¶3] This is precisely why engineers use what they call dimensional analysis — a method of chaining conversion factors together so that units cancel out cleanly. The process is always the same: write the given measurement, multiply by a conversion fraction, and check that the unwanted units cancel. However careful the calculation, the final sanity check — does this answer make sense? — is never skipped.

[¶4] The same logic applies to everyday problems. A swimmer asking how many laps equal a mile, a baker converting a recipe from cups to milliliters, a driver calculating fuel cost per kilometer — all are performing dimensional analysis. The bridge between a measurement you have and a measurement you need is always a conversion fact. The only question is whether to multiply or divide.
