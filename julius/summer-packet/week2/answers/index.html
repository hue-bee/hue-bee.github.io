Circles & Surface Area Problems

2 pts
#ProblemAnswerKey Work / Explanation
1 Sector with central angle 60° and radius r = 12 cm. (a) Find arc length. (b) Find sector area, in terms of π. L3 (a) 4π cm ≈ 12.57 cm  |  (b) 24π cm² ≈ 75.40 cm² Rule: Arc length = (θ/360) × 2πr  |  Sector Area = (θ/360) × πr²
(a) Arc = (60/360) × 2π(12) = (1/6) × 24π = 4π cm ≈ 12.57 cm
(b) Area = (60/360) × π(12)² = (1/6) × 144π = 24π cm² ≈ 75.40 cm²
⚠️ Trap: forgetting to reduce 60/360 to 1/6 before multiplying, or squaring the radius incorrectly in part (b)
2 Open-top planter box: l = 18 in, w = 9 in, h = 12 in. A circular drainage hole with radius 2 in is cut into the bottom. How much wood is needed? Leave π in your answer. L4 810 − 4π in² ≈ 797.43 in² Rule: Open-top surface area = lw + 2lh + 2wh (no top face) — then subtract the circular hole cut from the bottom
Bottom: 18 × 9 = 162 in²
2 long sides: 2 × 18 × 12 = 432 in²
2 short sides: 2 × 9 × 12 = 216 in²
Total surface: 162 + 432 + 216 = 810 in²
Hole area: π(2)² = 4π in² ≈ 12.57 in²
Wood needed: 810 − 4π ≈ 797.43 in²
⚠️ SHSAT Trap: "open-top" means NO top face — students who include a top face (adding another 162 in²) get 972 − 4π, which is incorrect. Also watch for students who add the hole area instead of subtracting it.

ELA — Reading: Informational Passage

Q1 Supporting Detail L2 B ¶1 states directly that "a circular cross-section carries the maximum volume of fluid for the minimum amount of pipe material." (A), (C), and (D) are not supported anywhere in the text.
Q2 Signal Words L2 B "However" always signals a contrast or limitation to the idea just presented — here, it pivots from praising the circle's efficiency (¶1) to noting its practical drawbacks (¶2). (A) is the opposite of what "however" signals. (C) and (D) misidentify the signal word's function.
⚠️ Trap: students who choose (A) because ¶2 still discusses circles positively later — but the word itself introduces the contrast, not a supporting example.
Q3 Author's Purpose / Contrast L3 B The Large Hadron Collider (27 km) and the human eye's pupil (millimeters) sit at opposite ends of the size spectrum, both relying on circular symmetry. The contrast demonstrates that the circle's usefulness is scale-independent. (A) and (D) invent a ranking not stated in the text. (C) man-made vs. natural is a surface-level observation, not the point of the comparison.
Q4 Main Idea L3 C ¶4's central claim is that learning circle formulas teaches "a mode of reasoning — from center to edge, from a single measurement to a prediction about the whole boundary" — a way of thinking used across many professions. (A) is too narrow (test-focused, not the paragraph's point). (B) and (D) are supporting details, not the main idea.
⚠️ Trap: (A) is tempting because the paragraph mentions formulas, but the paragraph explicitly says students are "not merely memorizing formulas."
Q5 Analysis / Extended Response L4 Open response — model answer below Model answer: "A circular gear transmits force more reliably than a square one because every point on its edge is the same distance from the center, so it rotates with constant, even contact. As the passage states, 'every radius is the same length, and that equality distributes force, pressure, and light perfectly.' A square gear's corners would create uneven distances from the center, causing the force to be applied inconsistently and the gear to skip or jam."
Award credit for any response that (1) connects equal radius length to smooth, even force transmission, and (2) quotes the "every radius is the same length" phrase (or equivalent) from ¶3.
⚠️ Trap: students who only restate that "circles are smoother" without explaining why (equal radius → equal distance → consistent force) should be redirected to the symmetry language in ¶3.
