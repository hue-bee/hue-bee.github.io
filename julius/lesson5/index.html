Part 1 · Triangle Types

The Three Triangle Families 🔺

Equilateral
All 3 sides equal
All 3 angles = 60°
"Equal everything"
3 × 60° = 180° ✓
Isosceles
2 equal sides → 2 equal angles
The base angles are always equal
"Iso = same legs"
apex + 2x = 180°
Right Triangle
One angle = 90°
Other two angles sum to 90°
The long side = hypotenuse
90 + a + b = 180°
If an isosceles triangle has an apex angle of 40°, what are its base angles? Don't calculate yet — what relationship do you already know that makes this solvable?
Part 1 · Isosceles Deep Dive

The Mirror Triangle — Isosceles Logic ⚖️

Here's the cheat: equal sides are always opposite equal angles. So if you know two sides are equal, what do you immediately know about two of the angles?
The Golden Isosceles Move:
1. Spot the two equal sides (tick marks)
2. The angles across from those equal sides are equal
3. Set up: apex + 2x = 180° → solve for x
Worked Example:
An isosceles triangle has a base angle of 65°. Find the apex.
→ apex + 65 + 65 = 180 → apex = 50°
⚠️ SHSAT Trap: "Equal sides" vs. "Equal angles"
Equal sides are across from equal angles — not adjacent to them. Don't confuse the sides with the angles they face.
Part 2 · Pythagorean Theorem

a² + b² = c² — The Ladder Secret 🪜

Imagine a ladder leaning against a wall. The ladder is 13 ft long, the base is 5 ft from the wall. Before you calculate: which measurement is the hypotenuse — and why?
The Rule: In every right triangle, the square of the longest side (hypotenuse) equals the sum of the squares of the two legs.

c is always across from the 90° angle.
a² + b² = c²
The Magic Triples (memorize these!):
3–4–5 → 9 + 16 = 25 ✓
5–12–13 → 25 + 144 = 169 ✓
8–15–17 · 7–24–25
Any multiple works: 6–8–10, 10–24–26…
⚠️ c² is always the LARGEST square
If a question gives you the hypotenuse, it goes on the RIGHT side of the equation: leg² + leg² = hyp²

Part 3 · Quadrilaterals

The Big Three Quadrilaterals ▭

Parallelogram
• Opposite sides parallel & equal
• Opposite angles equal
• Consecutive angles supplementary (sum = 180°)
• Diagonals bisect each other
∠A + ∠B = 180°
Rectangle
• ALL angles = 90°
• Opposite sides equal & parallel
• Diagonals equal in length
• A parallelogram with right angles
4 × 90° = 360°
Rhombus
• All 4 sides equal
• Opposite angles equal
• Diagonals bisect at 90° (perpendicular)
• A parallelogram with equal sides
diag₁ ⊥ diag₂
Is every rectangle a parallelogram? Is every parallelogram a rectangle? Think carefully — what's the key difference between them?

Part 3 · Quadrilateral Angles

Quadrilaterals Always Sum to 360° 🔲

Any quadrilateral can be cut into 2 triangles with a diagonal. How many degrees are in 2 triangles? What does that tell you about all 4 angles of any quadrilateral?
The Master Rule: Any quadrilateral's 4 angles always sum to 360° — just like a full rotation.
∠A + ∠B + ∠C + ∠D = 360°
For a parallelogram specifically:
• ∠A = ∠C (opposite angles equal)
• ∠A + ∠B = 180° (co-interior = supplementary)

Worked Example: A parallelogram has one angle of 70°. Find all four angles.
→ 70°, 110°, 70°, 110° (opposite pair each)
⚠️ 360° not 180° — quadrilaterals are NOT triangles!
A very common SHSAT error: students set up the angles of a quadrilateral = 180°. The correct sum is always 360°.

Part 4 · ELA — Poetry

Figurative Language — The Four Devices 🎭

Before I define anything: if I say "Julius's focus is a laser beam," am I saying he literally has a laser? What am I actually saying — and why is that more powerful than "he is focused"?
Simile: comparison using like or as
"The angle opened like a door."
Metaphor: direct comparison, no like/as
"Every angle is a grammar only the patient speak."
Personification: giving human traits to non-human things
"The triangle refused to yield its secret."
Imagery: language that triggers the senses (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell)
"The brass instrument cold against my eye."
Part 4 · Poetry Practice

Poem: "What the Rhombus Knows"

Read This Poem:

Color:

My father built fences the way theorems work —
each post leaning into the weight of the next,
nothing vertical, nothing accidental,
every angle chosen like a quiet argument.

He said: a square gives in too easily.
It stands rigid, proud, forgets that wind
is a proof that nothing stays perpendicular
forever.

A rhombus, though — a rhombus leans.
It knows the world is all pressure and yield,
that the diagonals of any life
cross somewhere in the middle.

I am learning to lean like that.
To hold my shape without holding my breath.

Part 4 · Discussion

Discuss & Debrief the Poem 💬

Q1 Answer: B — Metaphor
Wind is directly called a "proof" — no like or as → it's a metaphor. Note: wind isn't given human traits (not personification), and there's no sensory detail list (not imagery).
Q2 Answer: B — Forces that meet
The poet uses the geometric fact that rhombus diagonals bisect as a metaphor: our life's opposing tensions ("diagonals") inevitably cross. Answer C is literal — it describes the math, not the meaning.
Q3 Model Answer (L4)
The speaker uses the square to represent rigid thinking: "A square gives in too easily" suggests that inflexibility actually makes one weaker, not stronger. The rhombus, by contrast, represents adaptive strength: "A rhombus leans" — leaning without losing shape. The speaker values resilience over rigidity.
Did you notice the poem never uses the word "flexibility" or "resilience"? How does using geometry instead make the ideas feel different?
📋 Figurative Language Answer Protocol:
1. Name the device (simile, metaphor, etc.)
2. Quote the exact phrase from the poem
3. Explain what two things are being compared
4. State the effect: "This creates a feeling of ___"
⚠️ Simile vs. Metaphor — the like/as test
If you see like or as making the comparison → simile. If the two things are just fused together without those words → metaphor. This is the #1 SHSAT figurative language test.

Poem: "The Geese Again"

The geese crossed at forty-five degrees
above the river, precise as theorems,
their bodies solving for the wind
in a language older than any proof.

My student asked me once
why angles have to add to what they add to.
I said: because the universe insists.
She wrote it down like it meant something.

Maybe it did. The geese don't ask
why the updraft bends at just that pitch —
they lean into the angle that saves them
and call it instinct instead of math.

I've been teaching thirty years.
Every October the geese remind me
that understanding and knowing
are two different migrations.

Part 4 · Poem Comparison

Poem 2 Debrief & Compare Both Speakers 🔍

Q4 Answer: C — Personification
"Solving" is a human mathematical act given to geese. They are given the trait of doing math — that's personification, not imagery or simile.
Q5 Answer: B — Lived vs. Conscious
The geese "know" instinctively — they don't ask why. The student has to "understand" consciously. The poem suggests knowing is deeper and more embodied than verbal understanding.
Q6 Model Answer (L4)
The simile compares the geese's flight to theorems (X = geese's precision; Y = mathematical theorems). This suggests that math is not invented but discovered — it exists in nature, as precise and inevitable as the geese's migration path. The comparison elevates geometry to something ancient and universal.
Poem 1 speaker uses a rhombus to talk about personal resilience. Poem 2 speaker uses geese flying at 45° to talk about teaching. What is similar about what both speakers are really saying?
The Connecting Thread: Both poems use geometry/math as a lens for understanding human experience. Both speakers are people who have learned something about life through the process of learning math.
SHSAT Comparison Question Format:
"Both speakers…" questions expect you to find a theme that exists in BOTH poems. Don't describe each separately — find the overlap. Usually: the theme, the tone, or the speaker's purpose.
