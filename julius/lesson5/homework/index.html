Part D — ELA: Figurative Language in Poetry

6 Questions · ~15 min

Read each poem twice. Annotate: circle any simile/metaphor/personification, underline vivid imagery. Use the 3-step protocol: Name it → What's compared → Effect.

Simile: comparison using like or as
Metaphor: direct comparison, no like/as
Personification: human traits given to non-human things

Poem 1 — "The Surveyor's Daughter"

My mother read the land like a sentence,
each hill a clause, each fence line a comma
pausing the eye before the long horizon —
she measured the world and found it honest.

A right angle, she told me, never lies.
It is the one promise the ground keeps:
that up and across will always meet
at a corner you can trust your weight to.

I have made a life of crooked rooms,
of walls that lean like tired arguments.
But when I square a doorway true at last,
I hear her voice come level through the wood.

Poem 2 — "Parallelogram"

We were never a rectangle, you and I —
no easy corners, no four matching angles.
We leaned. We tilted toward each other's weight
the way two opposite sides agree to stay parallel
no matter how far the road slants underneath them.

People wanted us square. They wanted proof.
But a parallelogram keeps its own quiet law:
what is opposite is equal; what is adjacent
gives a little, sums to one straight line of peace.

So let them measure us and shake their heads.
Our diagonals still cross. They always have.
That crossing is the only center we have needed.
