Part 1 · Angle Types

The Four Angle Families

I show you an angle that looks like a slanted "L." Before measuring, how do you know it's obtuse — not acute?
Memory shortcut:
  • Acute — "a cute little angle" (small, sharp)
  • Right — the corner square symbol □ tells you
  • Obtuse — "big and obtuse" (wider than a corner)
  • Straight — looks exactly like a flat line
⚠️ SHSAT Trap
Diagrams are rarely drawn to scale. A 91° angle can look almost identical to a 90° angle — always use algebra, not your eyes!
Part 1 · Angle Pairs

Complementary & Supplementary — The Pair Rules

🧩
Complementary = "Complete the Corner"
Two angles are complementary if they sum to 90°. Together, they fill a right angle — like two puzzle pieces completing a corner.
∠A + ∠B = 90°
If ∠A = 37°, what is its complement? How did you find it?
📏
Supplementary = "Straighten the Line"
Two angles are supplementary if they sum to 180°. Together, they lie flat on a straight line — like two half-steps that meet in the middle.
∠A + ∠B = 180°
If one angle is 3× its supplement, can you set up the equation before solving?
⚠️ Memory Hook: "C" comes before "S" — 90 comes before 180. Complementary → 90, Supplementary → 180.
Part 2 · Lines & Verticals

The X-Frame & the Straight Line

Two straight lines cross. Without measuring, what can you say for certain about angles a and c? Why?
Vertical Angles: Angles across from each other at an intersection are always equal.

Angles on a Line: Angles that together form a straight line always sum to 180°.

Put together: if you know one angle in an X, you know all four!
Vertical angles: a = c  (and b = d)
Line angles: a + b = 180°
⚠️ SHSAT Trap: "Linear Pair"
A "linear pair" is just two adjacent angles on a straight line. They always sum to 180° — this is one of the most-tested facts on the SHSAT.

Part 3 · Triangle Rule

Every Triangle Sums to 180° 🔺

Imagine tearing the three corners off a triangle and placing them side by side along a line. What do you think happens? What angle do they make together?
The big rule: No matter what the triangle looks like — skinny, fat, tall, flat — its three interior angles always add to exactly 180°. This is your most powerful triangle tool.
∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180°
Worked Example:
Two angles in a triangle are 55° and 72°. Find the third.
→ 55 + 72 + ? = 180 → ? = 53°
Part 3 · Exterior Angles

The Exterior Angle Shortcut ⚡

If the exterior angle = 115°, and one remote interior angle = 48°, how would you find the other remote interior angle?
When you extend a side of a triangle, you create an exterior angle. Here's the trick:

Exterior angle = sum of the two non-adjacent ("remote") interior angles.

You don't need the third angle at all — just skip straight to the shortcut!
ext = a + b  (the two far angles)
⚠️ Why it works
The exterior angle and the adjacent interior angle are supplementary (sum = 180°). So: ext = 180 − c = a + b. Two paths, same answer!

Part 4 · ELA — Poetry

Who's Talking? What Do They Feel? 🎭

Imagine reading a poem where the speaker says "I watched them take my grandfather's chair." Before you read another word — what can you already guess about the tone?
Key distinction: The speaker is not the author. A poem by a 60-year-old writer might have a 10-year-old child as its speaker. Read the words, not the author's biography.

Part 4 · Poetry Practice

Poem: "The Surveyor's Lines"

Read This Poem:

My grandfather measured everything in angles —
the lean of a fence post, the pitch of a roof,
the exact degree at which a hawk rode the wind.
He said the world was made of triangles.

I was twelve and thought him foolish,
until I stood where he once stood,
a transit scope pressed to my eye,
and the whole hillside unfolded in degrees.

Now I carry his brass instrument
and read each morning's shadows like he did —
every angle a kind of grammar
only the patient learn to speak.

The world, I know now, is still made of triangles.
I just took longer than he did to see them.

Part 4 · Discussion

Discuss & Debrief the Poem

For Q1: Which lines in the poem tell you the speaker is NOT the grandfather — not the twelve-year-old present self — but someone older looking back?
For Q2: The speaker says "I was twelve and thought him foolish." Does feeling foolish about someone = bitterness? Or is there a warmer emotion here?
For Q3: The word "grammar" is unusual for a poem about measuring. Why would the poet compare angles to grammar — what do they have in common?
🎯 The SHSAT Poetry Checklist
  1. Who is speaking? (Find evidence in the poem.)
  2. What is the tone? (Use a precise word — not just "sad" or "happy.")
  3. Which two lines prove the tone? Quote them exactly.
  4. Watch for contrast: past vs. present, young vs. old, doubt vs. wisdom.
⚠️ Tone Trap: "Too Extreme"
"Bitter," "furious," "resentful" — these are too extreme for this poem. The speaker is mildly regretful, but ultimately warm. Wrong answer choices always push the emotion too far.
Part 4 · Poetry Practice 2

Poem: "Field Notes, Late October"

Read This Poem:

The geese crossed at forty-five degrees
above the river, precise as theorems,
their bodies solving for the wind
in a language older than any proof.

My student asked me once
why angles have to add to what they add to.
I said: because the universe insists.
She wrote it down like it meant something.

Maybe it did. The geese don't ask
why the updraft bends at just that pitch —
they lean into the angle that saves them
and call it instinct instead of math.

I've been teaching thirty years.
Every October the geese remind me
that understanding and knowing
are two different migrations.

Part 4 · Debrief

Debrief & Poem Comparison

For Q5: The geese don't study math, yet they "solve for the wind." What is the poet saying about the relationship between nature and mathematical truth?
For Q6: Why does the poet use the word "migrations" — a word about movement and distance — to describe understanding vs. knowing? What does the journey metaphor add?
Both poems involve someone who once didn't see something and later did. How does the tone of this teacher differ from the surveyor's grandchild?
🎯 Two-Poem Tone Comparison
"The Surveyor's Lines" — nostalgic, warm acceptance (came to see what grandfather saw)
"Field Notes, Late October" — reflective, humble wonder (thirty years teaching; still learning)
⚠️ SHSAT Pattern: "Two Different ___s"
When a poem ends by contrasting two seemingly similar things — like "understanding and knowing" — the contrast IS the main idea. The whole poem builds toward it. Look for it.
Geometry connection: Both poems weave math into their imagery — angles, theorems, triangles. On the SHSAT, connecting imagery to theme earns the hardest points.
