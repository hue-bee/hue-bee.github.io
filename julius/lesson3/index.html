Part 1 · Circles

Anatomy of a Circle

If you know the radius, what's the quickest way to find the diameter?
Three things to memorize:
  • Radius (r) — center to edge
  • Diameter (d) — edge to edge through center; d = 2r
  • Circumference (C) — the full boundary (like a fence)
⚠️ SHSAT Trap #1
Problems often give you diameter — but formulas need radius. Always halve first!
Part 1 · Circles

Fence vs. Carpet — The Two Formulas

🏃
Circumference = the fence
How far would you walk around the circle? Use when the problem mentions distance around, perimeter, or wrapping.
C = 2πr  =  πd
If r = 7, what is C? Leave in terms of π.
🪣
Area = the carpet
How much inside the circle? Use when the problem mentions covering, painting, filling, or space inside.
A = πr²
If d = 10, what is A? (Careful — what's r first?)
⚠️ SHSAT Trap #2
In A = πr², the exponent only applies to r — not to π. π stays as π (or 3.14). Students who write (πr)² double-square π and get 2–4× too large.

Part 2 · Arc & Sector

The Pizza Slice Trick 🍕

If a pizza has 8 equal slices and you take 1 slice — what fraction of the whole pizza do you have?
Key insight: A sector is just a fraction of the full circle.
That fraction = θ ÷ 360 (where θ is the central angle in degrees).
Arc Length = (θ/360) × 2πr
Sector Area = (θ/360) × πr²
⚠️ Trap: "Minor arc" vs. "Major arc"
If the angle given is >180°, use that angle directly. The minor arc uses the smaller angle.
Part 2 · Practice

Arc & Sector Problems — Three Levels

Problem 4L2

Problem 5L3

Problem 6L4

Part 3 · Surface Area

Unfold the Box 📦

If you cut open a cardboard box and flatten it, what shapes do you see?
A rectangular prism has 6 faces — 3 pairs of identical rectangles.
Surface area = add up all 6 faces.
SA = 2lw + 2lh + 2wh
Memory trick: "2 of each pair" — length×width, length×height, width×height.
⚠️ SA ≠ Volume!
Surface area is in square units (cm²). Volume is in cubic units (cm³). They are different formulas entirely.
Part 3 · Practice

Surface Area Problems — Three Levels

Problem 7L2
A cube has side length s = 7 cm. Find its total surface area. Hint: all 6 faces are identical squares.
Problem 8L3
An open-top box has l = 8, w = 5, h = 3. Find the surface area of the material needed (no top face).
Problem 9L4
A solid rectangular prism has l = 10, w = 6, h = 4. A 2 × 2 square hole is cut completely through the top and bottom faces. Find the total exposed surface area (include the 4 inner walls of the tunnel; exclude the hole openings on top and bottom).

Part 4 · ELA

The "Zoom Out" Strategy 🔭

You see this detail: "The Brooklyn Bridge used 14,680 miles of steel wire." What could the author be trying to PROVE?
Worked Example:
Detail: "Students scoring in top geometry quartile earn 23% more."
→ Proves: geometry education has real-world economic value.
→ Main Idea: Early math instruction is a long-term investment in students' futures.
⚠️ "Too Narrow" Trap
Main idea choices that simply restate one detail are wrong. The correct answer must cover the whole passage.
Part 4 · Practice

Passage + Socratic Questions

Read This Passage:

[¶1] For decades, educators debated whether geometry could be taught meaningfully to young students. The conventional view held that abstract spatial thinking was too sophisticated for children under twelve. Then, in 2009, a landmark study overturned that assumption. Researchers found that children who learn geometry early score 23% higher on spatial reasoning tests in high school — and that the advantage compounds over time, showing up in college-level STEM enrollment as well.

[¶2] Yet access is far from equal. Students in low-income schools receive 40% fewer hours of math instruction per year — not due to curriculum design, but chronic understaffing and budget cuts that force teachers to compress or skip geometry units entirely. A 2022 survey found that fewer than half of Title I middle schools offered any dedicated geometry lessons before eighth grade. Experts warn that this instructional gap is widening, not narrowing.

[¶3] History reminds us that math is never purely theoretical. When John Roebling designed the Brooklyn Bridge in the 1870s, his team relied on precise geometric calculations to place 14,680 miles of steel wire under the exact tension the design required. A single miscalculation could have been catastrophic. Every formula those engineers used is the same geometry taught — or in too many classrooms, not taught — today.

[¶4] The stakes, then, are not abstract. When a student misses early geometry instruction, she loses more than a unit on circles and angles. She loses the foundation that connects classroom math to bridges, satellites, medical imaging, and the physical world she will one day help build.

Part 4 · Discussion

Discuss & Debrief the Questions

For Q1: The passage now has four paragraphs. Which answer choice must cover all four — not just the opening paragraph?
For Q2: The word "tone" asks about the author's attitude, not just the facts. Look at word choices like "widening, not narrowing" — do those words sound neutral or concerned?
For Q3 (L4): The student's claim says the bridge "proves math is difficult." Does ¶3 say it is difficult, or does it say something else? Use ¶4 as a second piece of evidence.
🎯 The 3-Question Mental Checklist
  1. Does this answer cover the whole passage, or just one part?
  2. Is this answer stated or implied? (implied = valid!)
  3. Is there a word that makes this answer too extreme? (always, never, only)
⚠️ Extreme Language Trap
SHSAT wrong answers often use "always," "never," "only," "all," or "none." These overstatements are almost always wrong — even if the rest of the sentence sounds correct.
Part 4 · Passage 2

Architecture & Circles Passage

Read This Passage:

[¶1] When Roman engineers completed the Pantheon around 125 CE, they embedded a mathematical perfection that would not be surpassed for over a thousand years. The building's interior height equals its diameter exactly — both measuring 142 feet — creating a sphere of empty space that could theoretically contain a perfect globe. A circular opening at the very top, called the oculus, is the structure's only source of natural light. Rather than weaken the dome, the oculus actually distributes stress more evenly across the concrete shell — a geometric insight that modern engineers still study.

[¶2] Centuries later, architect Frank Lloyd Wright applied similar thinking when he designed the Guggenheim Museum in New York. Its circular plaza measures 76 feet in diameter, and Wright insisted the shape was not merely aesthetic. He argued that the circle was "the most human of shapes" because it eliminates corners, forcing everyone inside to face one another rather than turn away. Modern behavioral research supports his intuition: office teams working in circular conference rooms produce 18% more collaborative decisions than those in rectangular rooms, according to a 2018 study from Columbia University.

[¶3] The circle's influence does not stop at famous buildings. Satellite dishes are parabolic circles because the shape focuses incoming signals to a single point. Stadium running tracks are ovals — elongated circles — engineered so that every runner travels the same total distance regardless of lane. Water pipes are circular in cross-section because, for a given perimeter, a circle encloses more area than any other shape, maximizing flow while minimizing material.

[¶4] What the Pantheon, the Guggenheim, and a household drainpipe share is this: their designers did not choose the circle for decoration. They chose it because the mathematics of circles — the relationship between radius, circumference, and area — makes the circle the most efficient, the most stable, and, perhaps, the most human shape in the built world.
