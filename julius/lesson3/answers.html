Part A — Circles: Circumference & Area (Problems 1–6)

6 pts
#ProblemAnswerKey Work / Explanation
1 Circumference, r = 7 m L2 14π m C = 2πr = 2 × π × 7 = 14π m
2 Area, diameter = 18 ft L2 81π ft² r = 18 ÷ 2 = 9 ft  → A = πr² = π × 9² = 81π ft²  ⚠️ Watch: using d instead of r in the formula
3 C = 20π → find area L3 100π cm² Step 1: C = 2πr → 20π = 2πr → r = 10 cm  |  Step 2: A = π × 10² = 100π cm²  ⚠️ Watch: squaring before finding r
4 Annulus: outer D=16, inner r=3 L3 55π cm² Outer R = 16 ÷ 2 = 8 cm  → Shaded A = π(8²) − π(3²) = 64π − 9π = 55π cm²  ⚠️ Watch: forgetting to halve D for the outer radius
5 Sector area: 90°, r = 10 ft L2 25π ft² A = (90/360) × π × 10² = ¼ × 100π = 25π ft²
6a Arc length: 120°, r = 9 cm L3 6π cm Arc = (120/360) × 2π × 9 = ⅓ × 18π = 6π cm
6b Sector perimeter: arc + 2 radii L3 (6π + 18) cm Perimeter = arc + r + r = 6π + 9 + 9 = (6π + 18) cm  ⚠️ Watch: forgetting to add the two straight sides

Part B — Surface Area (Problems 7–12)

6 pts
#ProblemAnswerKey Work / Explanation
7 SA of cube, s = 8 in L2 384 in² SA = 6s² = 6 × 8² = 6 × 64 = 384 in²
8 SA of prism: l=6, w=4, h=5 cm L2 148 cm² SA = 2lw + 2lh + 2wh = 2(6×4) + 2(6×5) + 2(4×5) = 48 + 60 + 40 = 148 cm²
9 Open-top box: l=10, w=6, h=4 in L3 188 in² Bottom: 10×6=60  |  2 long sides: 2×(10×4)=80  |  2 short sides: 2×(6×4)=48  → Total = 60+80+48 = 188 in²  ⚠️ Watch: including the missing top face
10 Painting room: l=14, w=10, h=8; 4 walls + ceiling, 50 ft²/can L4 8 cans Ceiling: 14×10 = 140 ft²  |  4 walls: 2(14×8)+2(10×8) = 224+160 = 384 ft²  → Total = 524 ft²  → 524 ÷ 50 = 10.48 → round UP → 11 cans  ⚠️ Always round UP for paint cans
11 Circular track d=400 m, 3 laps L3 1200π m ≈ 3,768 m 1 lap = C = πd = 400π m  → 3 laps = 3 × 400π = 1200π m  ≈ 1200 × 3.14 = 3,768 m
12 Open-top aquarium: l=24, w=12, h=16; drain r=1.5 in L4 (1200 − 2.25π) in² Open-top SA: 24×12 + 2(24×16) + 2(12×16) = 288 + 768 + 384 = 1440 in²  |  Drain: π(1.5²) = 2.25π in²  → Glass = (1440 − 2.25π) in²  ⚠️ Watch: using full SA instead of open-top

Part C — ELA Passage 1: Author's Purpose & Tone (Q1–Q6)

6 pts

Passage: "The Hidden Circle: How Geometry Shapes the Way We Build"

Q#SkillAnswerWhy This Answer / Tutor Explanation
Q1 Author's Purpose B The passage consistently argues the circle is chosen for functional reasons across architecture, biology, and design — this is a persuasive/explanatory purpose. (A) is too narrow; (C) misreads the Snow example; (D) is only one paragraph.
Q2 Hook / Purpose of Example A Snow's cholera map is a real-world, life-saving application of circle geometry (radius, distance from center). The author uses it to hook the reader by proving geometry isn't just academic. ⚠️ Watch: (C) confuses entertainment with purpose.
Q3 Signal Words A "Makes it uniquely suited to" means the property described (equal distances from center) causes the circle to work well for those tasks — classic cause-effect. (B) contrast would require a word like "however"; (C) sequence needs "first/then".
Q4 Author's Tone B ¶5 says students learning formulas are "learning to read the language that nature uses when it is in perfect balance" — this is clearly inspiring/encouraging, elevating routine learning to something meaningful. (A), (C), (D) all contradict the warmth of this framing.
Q5 Text Structure B ¶1 = hook (Snow), ¶2 = circle's defining property, ¶3 = contrast (when circles are NOT used), ¶4 = examples where circles are the only choice, ¶5 = student relevance. This matches "hook → property → contrast → examples → relevance." (A) chronological only partially fits.
Q6 Evidence Evaluation C ¶4 explicitly states: "The circle, in these cases, was not decorative. It was the only shape that would work." This directly contradicts the student's claim. (B) is wrong because ¶4 makes no mention of aesthetics at all. ⚠️ This is a classic SHSAT trap — always re-read the paragraph before choosing.

Part C2 — ELA Passage 2: Inference & Evidence (Q7–Q12)

6 pts

Passage: "Circles in Nature: Why the Universe Prefers Curves"

Q#SkillAnswerWhy This Answer / Tutor Explanation
Q7 Central Claim B ¶5 directly states the central claim: "the circle is the shape of equilibrium" and it appears throughout nature whenever forces balance perfectly. (A) is about man-made shapes — the passage is about nature. (C) and (D) are supporting details, not the main claim.
Q8 Signal Words C "As a result" is a cause-effect signal. The previous sentence describes equal tension pulling inward → As a result, the bubble takes the shape that minimizes surface area. (A) contrast = "however/but"; (B) definition = "which means"; (D) sequence = "then/next".
Q9 Factual Inference (¶3) B ¶3 directly states Kepler was thrilled because "the ellipse is still a close cousin of the circle, and its mathematical properties are just as elegant." (A) and (C) are not stated in the text. (D) is the opposite of the passage's suggestion — ellipses are more complex, not simpler.
Q10 Author's Tone B ¶5 frames formula-learning as "learning to read the language that nature uses when it is in perfect balance" — inspiring and elevating. (A) is wrong: the author says students are NOT just memorizing. (C) and (D) contradict the warm, motivational register of the paragraph.
Q11 Best Evidence C The strongest evidence for "nature's default shape" comes from multiple concrete natural examples (soap bubbles, blood cells, tree rings, iris) all caused by equal forces — this directly supports the claim with physical evidence from the natural world. (A) is about children's drawings (human behavior, not nature). (B) actually introduces an exception (ellipses). (D) is naming a mathematical property, not proving the claim.
Q12 Multi-Paragraph Evidence Eval. B ¶3 does show an imperfection (orbits are ellipses, not perfect circles) — so the student's claim is partially supported. However, ¶5 argues that geometry still captures nature's equilibrium principle perfectly, which contradicts the claim's full scope. (A) overstates; (C) ignores ¶3's concession about ellipses; (D) is factually wrong — both paragraphs are highly relevant. ⚠️ This is the highest-difficulty question — reward students who cite both paragraphs.
