🌉 The Bridge Method

3 Steps — Every Time

1
WRITE the conversion fact
e.g. 1 foot = 12 inches
2
DECIDE: multiply or divide?
Smaller unit? → multiply. Bigger unit? → divide.
3
SANITY CHECK the label
Fewer big units, more small units — does your answer make sense?
Worked example: 36 inches → feet. Step 1: 1 ft = 12 in. Step 2: going UP → divide. Step 3: 36 ÷ 12 = 3 feet ✓ (fewer feet than inches ✓)

🔗 Content · Multi-Step Conversions

Chaining Two Bridges

💬 What if there's no direct conversion fact? You chain bridges:
yards → feet → inches  (2 bridges, both going down → multiply twice)
Example: 4 yards → inches
Step 1: 4 yd × 3 = 12 ft  →  Step 2: 12 ft × 12 = 144 in
Example: 2 hours → seconds
Step 1: 2 hr × 60 = 120 min  →  Step 2: 120 × 60 = 7,200 sec

🌍 Content · Metric System

Always Powers of 10

Prefix Chart
kilo- = × 1,000  →  1 km = 1,000 m
centi- = ÷ 100  →  1 m = 100 cm
milli- = ÷ 1,000  →  1 m = 1,000 mm
All 4 Metric Families
Length: km → m → cm → mm
Mass: kg → g → mg
Volume: L → mL
Rule: × 10/100/1000 going down, ÷ going up

⚠️ Content · SHSAT Traps

The 4 Biggest Mistakes

🪤 Trap 1 — Flipped fraction: Writing 1 ft / 12 in when you need 12 in / 1 ft
🪤 Trap 2 — Wrong operation: Dividing when you should multiply (going DOWN means ×)
🪤 Trap 3 — Decimal time: 230 min ≠ 2.3 hours. 230 ÷ 60 = 3 hr 50 min, not 3.8 hrs
🪤 Trap 4 — Missing label: Getting the right number but the wrong unit — always write the unit!

📖 Reading Passage · 4 min · Annotate as you read

The New York City Water System

Paragraph 1. New York City's water supply is one of the largest unfiltered surface water systems in the world. Every day, more than 1 billion gallons travel from upstate reservoirs through tunnels and pipes — some buried 800 feet underground — before reaching city taps. The system spans 19 reservoirs across a 2,000-square-mile watershed.

Paragraph 2. For decades, however, the aging tunnels posed a serious problem. City Tunnel No. 1, built in 1917, had never been shut down for inspection. Engineers feared that closing it — even briefly — could cut off water to millions of people. The infrastructure was quietly failing, and a major repair was long overdue.

Paragraph 3. As a result, the city launched one of the most ambitious infrastructure projects in American history: City Water Tunnel No. 3. Construction began in 1970 and took over 40 years to complete. The new tunnel stretches 60 miles and runs as deep as 800 feet below street level, allowing engineers to finally inspect — and repair — the older tunnels without cutting off the city's water.

Paragraph 4. Today, the water flows reliably to over 8 million New Yorkers. The completion of Tunnel No. 3 didn't just solve the immediate crisis — it secured the city's water supply for the next 100 years.
